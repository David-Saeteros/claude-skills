# Section-Specific Drafting Guides

Reference file for the academic-writing skill. Load when drafting a specific section.

---

## Introduction / Background

**Purpose:** Establish the problem, justify the study, and state the contribution.

**Structure (empirical article):**
1. Hook — establish the phenomenon or problem (1–2 sentences, specific not generic)
2. Gap statement — what is unknown, contested, or under-studied
3. Consequence of the gap — why it matters
4. Present study — what this paper does (not "we investigated X" but "this study tests X")
5. Contribution — what the paper adds to the field
6. Road map (optional) — one sentence overview of paper structure

**Common mistakes:**
- Opening with "Many researchers have studied X…" — too broad, bury the hook
- Gap stated but not justified (so what?)
- Contribution buried in paragraph 4 instead of foregrounded
- Road map sentence at the end of lit review rather than intro

**Thesis / grant variation:** Expand gap section substantially; contribution becomes aims/objectives;
road map becomes chapter overview or specific aims page.

## Literature Review / Background Section

**Purpose:** Position the study within existing knowledge; justify the theoretical framework.

**Structure:**
1. Organise thematically, not chronologically (exception: when history of a construct IS the argument)
2. Each subsection = one conceptual strand, ending with how it connects to the present study
3. Transition sentences must do logical work: not "Another study found…" but "While X established
   Y, the mechanism underlying Z remains unclear, motivating the current investigation."

**Common mistakes:**
- Annotation list disguised as a review (Study A found X. Study B found Y. Study C found Z.)
- Hedging every claim with "however" — undermines the argument
- Missing synthesis: studies catalogued but not compared or evaluated
- Neglecting contradictory evidence

**Systematic review variation:** Must address search strategy, inclusion/exclusion criteria, PRISMA
compliance, and risk-of-bias assessment. These are structural requirements, not optional additions.

## Methods

**Purpose:** Enable replication. Every decision that affects the results must be documented.

**Subsections (empirical quantitative):**
- Participants / Sample
  - N, recruitment method, inclusion/exclusion criteria, compensation
  - Demographic descriptives (APA: report M and SD for continuous vars; frequencies for categorical)
  - Power analysis (a priori preferred; post-hoc acceptable with caveats)
- Design
  - Between/within/mixed; experimental/observational/quasi-experimental
  - Randomisation and blinding (if applicable)
- Materials / Measures
  - Each instrument: name, citation, subscales, scoring, reliability (Cronbach's α or ω)
  - Stimulus materials if applicable
- Procedure
  - Chronological walkthrough of what participants experienced
  - Ethics approval statement
- Analytic strategy
  - Software, version, packages
  - Model specification before results

**Common mistakes:**
- Passive voice that conceals who made decisions ("participants were assigned" — by whom? randomly?)
- Missing reliability coefficients for scales
- Procedure written from researcher perspective, not participant experience
- Analysis plan described in results instead of methods

**Qualitative variation:** Add reflexivity statement, saturation criteria, and member-checking
or other validity procedures. Thematic analysis requires: transcription approach, coding procedure
(deductive/inductive), unit of analysis, inter-rater reliability if applicable.

## Results

**Purpose:** Report findings without interpretation.

**Rules:**
- Every statistic reported must be complete: test statistic, df, p-value, effect size, CI where
  applicable. APA 7th format: *F*(2, 87) = 4.32, *p* = .016, η²ₚ = .09, 95% CI [.01, .19]
- Tables and figures are primary; text summarises, does not repeat them verbatim
- Results are ordered to match the stated hypotheses / research questions
- Non-significant findings are reported, not suppressed

**Common mistakes:**
- "The results were significant" — of what? at what threshold?
- Effect sizes absent (now required by most journals)
- Interpretation creeping into results ("surprisingly", "this suggests")
- Table presented but not referenced in text

## Discussion

**Purpose:** Interpret findings in light of theory and prior literature; acknowledge limits; state implications.

**Structure:**
1. Summary of main findings (1 paragraph, no new statistics)
2. Interpretation paragraph per key finding — link back to literature reviewed in intro
3. Unexpected or null findings addressed directly (never buried)
4. Limitations — specific, not performative ("future research is needed" is not a limitation)
5. Implications — theoretical and/or practical, commensurate with the actual findings
6. Conclusion — 1 paragraph, does not introduce new material

**Common mistakes:**
- Discussion = extended results section (still reporting, not interpreting)
- Overclaiming: "This study proves…" / "These findings demonstrate that…" (use "suggest", "indicate",
  "are consistent with")
- Underclaiming: valid findings hedged into meaninglessness
- Limitations section too brief or generic
- Conclusion longer than necessary; repeats discussion

## Abstract

**Structured abstract (most empirical journals):**
- Background / Objective (1–2 sentences)
- Method (2–3 sentences: N, design, measures)
- Results (2–3 sentences: key findings with statistics if word count allows)
- Conclusions / Implications (1–2 sentences)

**Unstructured abstract:**
- Same content, no subheadings, 150–250 words (check journal)
- Write last; never introduce content not in the paper

**Keywords:** 4–6 terms; avoid repeating words from the title; use MeSH or PsycINFO thesaurus terms
where available for discoverability.

## Grant Proposals

**Key difference from journal articles:** Reviewers are evaluating *potential*, not completed work.
Every section must answer: "Why will this work, and why will this team deliver it?"

**Specific aims page (NIH style):**
- Paragraph 1: significance and gap (3–4 sentences max — reviewers read this first)
- Paragraph 2: innovation (what is novel about the approach)
- Paragraph 3: central hypothesis and rationale
- Aims list: 2–4 aims, each with hypothesis and expected outcome
- Closing: impact statement

**Common mistakes:**
- Too much background, not enough hypothesis
- Aims that are outputs, not research questions ("Aim 1: collect data on X")
- Feasibility not addressed
- Preliminary data section undersells existing evidence

## Thesis / Dissertation Chapters

**Introduction chapter:** Broader than journal article intro — includes conceptual framework,
definition of key constructs, and chapter overview.

**Chapter-level introductions:** Each empirical chapter needs its own mini-intro (why this study,
how it fits the thesis arc).

**General discussion (final chapter):** Synthesises across all studies; addresses whether the
overall programme of research answered the overarching question; stronger implications section
than individual paper discussions.

**Common mistakes:**
- Treating the thesis as a stapled collection of papers without connective tissue
- Theoretical framework chapter that describes theories without applying them to the thesis question
- Conclusion that only summarises without synthesising
